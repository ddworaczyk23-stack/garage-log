# Warning lights — addendum (coolant temp + red brake light)

Status: usable for domain/playbooks.ts. Adds two light choices the original
03-warning-lights.md research didn't cover as their own top-level options.

## A. Coolant temperature light / gauge in the red

Sources agree this is one of the few lights with no "wait and see" branch:
continuing to drive risks warping the cylinder head or blowing the head
gasket within minutes. Standard guidance across CarParts.com, RepairPal, and
Christian Brothers Automotive: pull off safely, run the heater on full blast
(pulls heat off the engine), shut the engine off once stopped, and don't
restart until it's fixed.

Likely causes: coolant leak (hose, radiator, water pump) — most common;
failed cooling fan or fan clutch; stuck thermostat; failed water pump;
already-blown head gasket (less common as the FIRST sign, more often the
result of ignoring an earlier overheat).

Urgency: **fix-now**, verdict **tow** — this is the one dashboard light where
"drive carefully to the shop" is explicitly the wrong advice everywhere it's
discussed.

Cost (2025-2026):
- If caught early (hose/thermostat/fan): Indie $150–900 · Dealer $250–1,200.
- If it progressed to a head gasket: Indie/Dealer $1,500–3,500 typical,
  $2,475–3,246 average per RepairPal; up to $8,000 on complex engines or with
  head damage (+$400–800 machining, +$800–2,000 head replacement).

Shop-brief: "Temperature gauge/light indicated overheating. Please pressure-
test the cooling system, determine external vs. internal coolant loss, and
run a combustion-gas / head-gasket check if internal loss is suspected before
quoting a head-gasket repair."

## B. Red brake warning light (separate from the amber ABS light)

Sources (Brakes-4-Less, PowerStop, RepairPal, DirectBrakes) are consistent on
one point worth building a question around: **the single most common trigger
is a parking brake that's still engaged, or not fully released** — a false
alarm, not a fault. Once that's ruled out, the red light indicates the core
hydraulic brake system (fluid level, hydraulic pressure) or pads worn to
their built-in wear sensor — distinct from the amber ABS/traction light,
which is the anti-lock system only (normal brakes still work).

Likely causes (parking brake ruled out): low brake fluid from a leak or worn
pads (most common) · a hydraulic system fault (master cylinder, proportioning
valve) · pads worn to the built-in sensor.

Urgency: **fix-now**, verdict **tow** — brake fluid doesn't evaporate on its
own, so low fluid means a leak or genuinely worn-out pads; either way this is
the core braking system, not an assist feature.

Cost (2025-2026): fluid top-up alone is free/near-free if it's just topping
off (but the LEAK still needs finding); pad replacement $200–500 indie;
hydraulic leak / master cylinder repair $400–2,000+.

Shop-brief: "Red brake warning light on, parking brake confirmed released.
Please check brake fluid level and look for a leak, inspect pad wear sensors,
and test the hydraulic system before quoting a repair."
